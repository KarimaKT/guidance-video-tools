# /technical-learning-video

Use this skill before creating a technical explainer, product walkthrough, app demo, course video, learning series, or advisory education asset.

## Purpose

Turn a hard topic, source material, demo scenario, or product feature into a viewer-centered learning plan that can be reviewed and then rendered in CAT Video Tools.

The output should teach, orient, and make the viewer care. It should not be a lazy summary, implementation dump, or internal workflow checklist.

## Inputs to ask for

- Topic, product, feature, or app name
- Target viewer and job-to-be-done
- Problem, misconception, or pain the video should correct
- Source links, local files, screenshots, code samples, or demo clips
- Desired output type: short app demo, technical explainer, product walkthrough, or course/series
- Any required title-page direction, branding, or feature focus

## Required output

Produce a review-ready brief with:

1. Viewer and job-to-be-done
2. Hook/problem statement
3. Source-grounded summary with traceable source names or URLs
4. Misunderstandings to correct
5. Mental model and aha moment
6. Recommended format: demo, explainer, walkthrough, or series
7. Storyboard with scenes, narration intent, visual strategy, and review notes
8. Two-pass viewer review checklist
9. Post-render QA checklist

## App/demo opening standard

For any app, product, or tool demo, start with a product reveal before mechanics.

Preferred opening pattern:

> Introducing [app/product name]. We built this app to [solve problem] for [audience]. It helps you [feature/outcome 1], [feature/outcome 2], and [feature/outcome 3].

The opening must name the app, say what was built, identify the audience, explain the problem, and preview the main features before workflow details.

## Technical explainer standard

For technical explainers, design a learning experience rather than a content dump.

Include:

- learner persona
- starting misconception or pain
- mental model
- product primitives and data types
- decisions and tradeoffs
- concrete scenario or example
- memorable aha moment
- closing application: what the viewer can now do differently

If the source is too large, split it into a course-style sequence where each part stands alone and builds toward the whole.

## Two-pass review before rendering

Before rendering, review the proposed script twice from the viewer perspective.

### Pass 1: orientation and value

Check whether the viewer knows:

- what the topic/app/product is called
- what was built or what is being taught
- who it is for
- what problem it solves
- what features or outcomes they will see
- why the details matter

### Pass 2: engagement and usefulness

Check whether:

- the video creates a concrete pain, scenario, misconception, or outcome
- the narration teaches rather than lists facts
- there is at least one aha moment or memorable reframe
- the viewer knows what to do differently after watching
- visuals clarify the idea instead of filling time
- the script avoids administrative wording, prompt mirroring, and internal process details

If either pass is weak, revise before rendering.

## Visual and guided-tour standards

- One main idea per slide
- Generous padding and whitespace
- Short on-screen text; narration carries detail
- Strong titles that teach the point
- Diagrams, screenshots, timelines, comparisons, or staged reveals where useful
- Guided web tours should include visible step/time markers and narration context
- Avoid silent screen recordings inside a demo unless they are explicitly described as raw footage

## Post-render QA

After rendering, confirm:

- output files exist
- durations are plausible
- stitched duration matches scene duration within tolerance
- representative frames look clean
- title/context comes before mechanics
- transitions into/out of embedded clips are not abrupt
- audio and image continuity are intact
- the two pre-render reviews were completed

## Copilot Studio evaluation course pattern

For Copilot Studio evaluation learning videos, teach the lifecycle from a maker's shoes:

- scope the V1 value promise and acceptance contract
- create starter test sets and buckets
- choose expected answers, capabilities, graders, rubrics, and blind sets
- use analytics sessions and production evidence after launch
- operationalize quality only after the signal is trusted

Focus on what the maker should put in the test set, what evidence proves readiness, how to avoid overfitting, and how production usage becomes the next evaluation loop.
